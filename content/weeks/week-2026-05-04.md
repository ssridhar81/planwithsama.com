## LINKEDIN — POST 1 (MONDAY)

### Post
**Idea source:** content/ideas/linkedin-monday.md — "AI Agent Conference NYC May 4 - May 5 2026 : Day 1"

Day 1 of the AI Agent Conference in New York. One observation kept surfacing — across sessions, hallway conversations, and demos:

There is no playbook yet.

We've been deploying enterprise AI for years now. The patterns are mature. The procurement decks are polished. The change management frameworks are well-rehearsed. But the moment you move from AI assistants to AI agents — systems that act, decide, chain steps and operate with real autonomy — the playbook collapses.

And the people doing it well are doing it by trial and error. Out loud. In production. With real customers and real consequences.

That's not a criticism. It's an honest observation about where we are in the technology cycle. Agentic AI is not a more advanced version of generative AI. It's a different category of system, with different failure modes, different evaluation criteria, and a different relationship to the people deploying it.

The organisations getting traction aren't the ones with the most sophisticated theory. They're the ones with the highest tolerance for visible iteration. They're shipping. They're breaking things. They're learning in the open. And they are, as a result, building real institutional knowledge nobody else has access to yet.

The implication for AI strategy is uncomfortable. You cannot wait for the playbook to emerge. The playbook is being written, in real time, by the teams currently doing the work.

If your agentic AI strategy is "wait until best practice is clear" — you are choosing to be a follower in a category where there is currently no leader.

## LINKEDIN — POST 2 (THURSDAY)

### Post
**Idea source:** content/ideas/linkedin-thursday.md — "Agentify Software Infrastructure"

Something I've been noticing in the agentic AI conversation this week — particularly in software infrastructure.

We are producing code at an unprecedented rate. Coding agents have multiplied output. The dashboards look incredible. The velocity metrics are staggering.

But there is a quieter, more interesting bottleneck emerging underneath all of it: verification.

The act of checking, validating, testing, reviewing, debugging, and ultimately taking responsibility for what an agent has produced — that has not been automated away. It has, if anything, expanded. There is more code to verify than ever before. More changes to review. More edge cases to chase. More incidents to RCA at 2am.

And the cost of that verification work is not landing on the dashboards. It's landing on the cognitive load of the engineers and operators doing it.

Tools like Bits AI SRE for root cause analysis, or anomaly detection models like Datadog's Toto, are starting to address the operational side. They help. But they don't change the underlying truth: the person closing the loop on agent output is still a human, doing increasingly complex judgement work, carrying increasingly invisible cognitive load.

We talk a lot about agent productivity. We rarely measure the rising tax on the people responsible for verifying the output.

It's the new shape of an old pattern. AI generates more. Humans absorb the cognitive overhead of making sure it's right. And the absorption is invisible — until something goes wrong.

What would change if we measured cognitive load on verification work as carefully as we measure agent throughput?
