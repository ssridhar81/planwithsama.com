## LINKEDIN — POST 1 (MONDAY)

### Post
**Idea source:** content/ideas/linkedin-monday.md — "Scaling Agentic AI Across the Enterprise"

The economics of agentic AI are starting to separate serious deployments from experiments.

One observation I keep coming back to from conversations this past week: the cost of operating agent models at scale is not a line item many organisations fully priced in when they committed to agentic strategies. High token consumption, inference costs, orchestration overhead — the economics are fundamentally different from static model deployment, and they compound quickly at enterprise scale.

The organisations working through this have reached a similar conclusion: open source models are increasingly the play for the inference layer. Not because proprietary frontier models aren't extraordinary — they are — but because running agents end-to-end on premium token pricing is, for most enterprise use cases, not economically sustainable as volume scales.

The more interesting observation, though, is what "AI-native" actually demands from a business.

It is not a technology decision. It is a business model decision. The companies making real progress have CEO-level conviction that AI is not a tooling upgrade but a fundamental restructuring of how the business operates, develops and is managed. They are not adopting AI into existing workflows. They are building the company around the capabilities AI gives them.

That is a different orientation entirely. And it requires a different kind of management rigour — clear governance, fast iteration culture, and leaders willing to own the transformation publicly, not delegate it to a technology function.

The organisations that will make it in the agentic era are the ones who take that seriously at the top.

Are you thinking about agentic AI as a technology decision or a business model decision in your organisation?

## LINKEDIN — POST 2 (THURSDAY)

### Post
**Idea source:** content/ideas/linkedin-thursday.md — "Building Recurring, Governed And Embedded Enterprise Workflows by CrewAI"

Something I heard at the AI Agent Conference last week is worth sitting with.

One of the more honest observations — from a team actually shipping recurring, governed agentic workflows at scale — was this: the hardest part is not the engineering. It is the adoption.

Which sounds obvious. But it is worth being specific about what that means.

When you deploy an agent into an enterprise workflow, you are not asking people to use a new tool. You are asking them to extend trust to a system that acts — that makes decisions, initiates steps, and operates in the gaps between human oversight moments. That is a fundamentally different cognitive relationship than using AI as an assistant.

The friction point is rarely the technology. It is the person on the other end, already operating at full cognitive capacity, now also being asked to understand, monitor, verify and occasionally override an autonomous system they did not build and may not fully trust.

The companies achieving real adoption are building the flywheel from the other direction. They do not launch agents and wait for people to adapt. They identify where the agent can demonstrably reduce cognitive load first — in ways people feel immediately — and let adoption follow from relief rather than obligation.

That shift in framing changes everything. Governance, rollout sequencing, how success is measured in the early weeks. The question is not "how do we get people to use this?" It is "what is the first thing this removes from their plate that they will actually notice?"

It is not an engineering problem. It is a human systems problem.

What is the biggest behavioural — not technical — barrier you have encountered in getting agentic workflows to stick?
